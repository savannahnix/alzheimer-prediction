## Pipeline Overview

This project predicts Alzheimer’s disease progression using longitudinal MRI data transformed into deformation vector fields (DVFs).

The pipeline consists of:

1. **Data Loading** – Load and normalize longitudinal MRI-derived DVFs  
2. **Feature Extraction** – 3D CNN extracts spatial representations  
3. **Positional Encoding** – Inject temporal + spatial structure  
4. **Sequence Modeling** – Longformer (attention) + Mamba (state-space)  
5. **Pooling** – Aggregate patient-level representation  
6. **Survival Head** – Predict hazard rates at discrete time points  
7. **Interpolation & Evaluation** – Continuous survival curves + metrics  

This hybrid architecture captures both localized structural changes and long-range disease progression patterns over time.




### What Goes In, What Comes Out
- **Input:** A longitudinal sequence of 3D Deformation Vector Fields (DVFs). These are generated by VoxelMorph registering each visit's MRI scan to a standard template. Each scan is a 3D volume of shape `[3, 128, 128, 128]` (representing displacement vectors Δx, Δy, Δz for every voxel). A patient might have between 2 and 10 of these visits.
- **Output:** A discrete survival curve—specifically, five hazard rates representing the instantaneous risk of converting to dementia at months 12, 24, 36, 48, and 60.

## Architecture at a Glance

The entire flow from raw data to a survival prediction is split into distinct phases.

```
flowchart TD
    A[Raw 3D Scans: BxVx3x128x128x128] --> P0[Phase 0: Data Loading]
    P0 --> P1[Phase 1: BrainIAC 3D ResNet50\nSpatial Feature Extraction]
    P1 --> P2[Phase 2: Positional Encodings\nAdding Time and Space]
    P2 --> P3[Phase 3 & 4: Longformer & Mamba\nParallel Gated Fusion]
    P3 --> P5[Phase 5: PMA Pooling\nPatient Summary]
    P5 --> P6[Phase 6: TraCeR Survival Head\nHazard Prediction]
    P6 --> P7[Phase 7 & 8: Interpolation, Loss,\n& Evaluation]
```

## Phase 0: The Data Foundation
**File Locations:** `config/model_config.py`, `data/dvf_dataset.py`  
**Classes:** `ModelConfig`, `LongitudinalDVFDataset`

**Layman's Explanation:** 
Imagine trying to read ten encyclopedias at once; they are too heavy to hold. Since 3D brain scans are massive (~25MB each), we can't load the entire dataset into memory. Phase 0 is our librarian. It fetches the exact brain scans needed for a small batch of patients, pads them so everyone has the same number of "visits" (filling in blanks if someone missed an appointment), and normalizes the data so the model doesn't get overwhelmed by wildly different numerical scales.

**Technical Explanation & Tradeoffs:**
- **Inputs:** Raw DVF tensor files from disk.
- **Outputs:** A dictionary containing:
  - `dvf_sequence`: Tensor of shape `[Batch, V_max, 3, 128, 128, 128]`.
  - `time_deltas`: Months between visits.
  - `missing_mask`: Binary mask indicating real visits vs. padded visits.
  - `duration` & `event`: The survival targets (time to event/censoring, and a censoring indicator).
- **Tradeoffs:** Because memory is limited, we use a physical batch size of 2 (to fit in Apple Silicon MPS memory) but use **gradient accumulation** (simulating a batch size of 16 by accumulating gradients over 8 steps) to keep the training stable. We also use Welford's online algorithm to normalize the scans because computing the mean and variance of the whole dataset at once would cause an Out-Of-Memory (OOM) crash.

## Phase 1: Feature Extraction
**File Location:** `models/brainiac_extractor.py`  
**Classes:** `BrainIACFeatureExtractor`

**Layman's Explanation:** 
You can't show a person a 2-million-pixel 3D image and expect them to understand the whole thing instantly. They have to scan it chunk by chunk. Phase 1 is like a magnifying glass that scans the 3D brain, identifies the most important textures and shapes (like the shrinking of the hippocampus), and summarizes each brain scan into a grid of 512 "tokens" (high-level summaries). 

**Technical Explanation & Tradeoffs:**
- **Inputs:** `[Batch, V, 3, 128, 128, 128]` 
- **Outputs:** `[Batch, V, 512, d_model]` (where `d_model=512`, so 512 spatial tokens per visit).
- **How it works:** We use a Pretrained 3D ResNet50 (from MONAI's MedicalNet). It processes the images using 3D convolutions, shrinking the spatial dimensions by 16x. 
- **Tradeoffs & Justification:** 
  - *Why not ViT?* We use a ResNet50 instead of a Vision Transformer because MedicalNet provides robust pre-trained weights for 3D CNNs.
  - *Why GroupNorm?* We replace BatchNorm with GroupNorm because our batch sizes are tiny (just 2). BatchNorm fails completely with micro-batches, whereas GroupNorm normalizes across channel groups, independent of batch size.
  - *Why `no_max_pool`?* We skip the initial max pooling step. If we pooled, we'd compress the brain too much and lose fine-grained details needed to predict MCI conversion.

## Phase 2: Adding Time and Space
**File Locations:** `utils/positional_encoding.py`, `models/base.py`  
**Classes:** `CTLPE`, `SpatialSinusoidalPE`, `ModalityDropout`

**Layman's Explanation:** 
If you hand a detective 5 photos of a crime scene but don't tell them what order they were taken in, or what part of the room they show, they are useless. Neural networks are naturally "blind" to order. Phase 2 stamps every token with two pieces of information: "Where am I in the brain?" (Spatial Encoding) and "How many months has it been since the first scan?" (Temporal Encoding). We also sometimes "hide" a visit during training (Dropout) so the model learns to cope if a patient skips an appointment.

**Technical Explanation & Tradeoffs:**
- **Inputs:** `[Batch, V, 512, d_model]` 
- **Outputs:** `[Batch, V, 512, d_model]` (with added positional embeddings)
- **How it works:** 
  - **CTLPE (Continuous Temporal Learnable PE):** Patients visit at irregular intervals (e.g., month 0, 6, 24). Standard models assume evenly spaced data. CTLPE learns a linear projection of the *actual time delta*, allowing the model to respect the real passage of time.
  - **Spatial PE:** Standard fixed sinusoidal encodings that map the 3D grid.
- **Tradeoffs:** `ModalityDropout` intentionally drops entire visits with 25% probability. This slightly slows down initial training convergence but makes the model much more robust to missing data in clinical practice.

## Phase 3 & 4: Understanding Progression over Time (Parallel Fusion)
**File Locations:** `models/longformer_sequence.py`, `models/mamba_sequence.py`, `models/pipeline.py`  
**Classes:** `LongformerSequence`, `Mamba3DSequence`, `GatedFusion`

**Layman's Explanation:** 
Now that the model has 512 summaries per visit, for 10 visits, it has a total of 5,120 puzzle pieces. How do we see the progression of Alzheimer's across all pieces? 
We use two different "experts" looking at the data at the same time:
1. **The Longformer (The Detail-Oriented Expert):** Looks at specific connections between visits (e.g., comparing the hippocampus at Month 0 directly to Month 24).
2. **The Mamba (The Trend Expert):** Scans the whole sequence from start to finish, feeling the gradual momentum of the disease.
The model then dynamically blends their opinions using a "Gate" depending on who it thinks is right.

**Technical Explanation & Tradeoffs:**
- **Inputs:** `[Batch, V*512, d_model]`
- **Outputs:** `[Batch, V*512, d_model]`
- **How it works:** 
  - **Longformer:** Standard Transformers scale quadratically (5120^2 is too slow). Longformer uses a "sliding window" attention (size 512) to let tokens attend to their own visit, plus a few "global" separator tokens to communicate between visits.
  - **Mamba:** A Selective State Space Model. It scales linearly $O(N)$, which is incredibly fast and memory efficient. It excels at capturing smooth continuous dynamics over time.
  - **Gated Fusion:** Instead of just concatenating their outputs (which would double the memory footprint downstream), we use a learned gating mechanism `alpha`. Output = $\alpha \times Longformer + (1 - \alpha) \times Mamba$. 
- **Tradeoffs:** Running two sequence models is computationally expensive, but neurodegenerative progression contains both highly localized spatial signatures (best captured by Attention) and smooth temporal trajectories (best captured by State-Space Models). The gate allows the network to optimize this tradeoff per feature.

## Phase 5: Summarizing the Patient
**File Location:** `models/pooling.py`  
**Classes:** `PoolingByMultiheadAttention` (PMA)

**Layman's Explanation:** 
We still have 5,120 puzzle pieces. We need to boil this down into a single "Patient Profile" before we can make a prediction. Instead of just taking a simple average (which waters down important details), we use 8 "Seed" questions. These seeds act like specialized investigators asking: "Is there atrophy in region X?" or "Did things speed up in year 2?". They extract exactly what they need to create a final, dense 4,096-number summary.

**Technical Explanation & Tradeoffs:**
- **Inputs:** `[Batch, V*512, d_model]`
- **Outputs:** `[Batch, 4096]`
- **How it works:** PMA utilizes Multi-Head Attention. We initialize 8 learnable "seed" vectors. These seeds act as the Queries ($Q$), while the token sequence acts as Keys ($K$) and Values ($V$). 
- **Tradeoffs:** Mean pooling is fast but destroys variance. CLS tokens often fail in very long sequences. PMA adds slight computational overhead but ensures that only the most highly relevant tokens contribute to the final survival prediction.

## Phase 6: Predicting the Future (Survival Head)
**File Location:** `models/survival_head.py`  
**Classes:** `TraCeRSurvivalHead`

**Layman's Explanation:** 
This is the final decision-maker. It takes the "Patient Profile" and outputs 5 numbers: The hazard (risk) of getting dementia at year 1, year 2, year 3, year 4, and year 5. From these 5 numbers, we can draw a curve showing the probability of the patient remaining healthy over time.

**Technical Explanation & Tradeoffs:**
- **Inputs:** `[Batch, 4096]`
- **Outputs:** `hazards` of shape `[Batch, 1, 5]` (1 cause, 5 time points).
- **How it works:** A multi-layer perceptron (MLP) mapping the embedding down to discrete hazard rates using a Sigmoid activation. We clamp the outputs using `HAZARD_EPS` to prevent mathematical errors in log-space.
- **Tradeoffs vs. Baseline CoxPH:** 
  - Baseline CoxPH assumes *Proportional Hazards* (the risk ratio between two patients is constant over time). 
  - TraCeR makes no such assumption. It models non-proportional hazards, meaning it can recognize that Patient A might be at higher risk in year 1, but Patient B might overtake them in year 4.

## Phase 7 & 8: Interpolation, Loss, and Evaluation
**File Locations:** `utils/chi_interpolation.py`, `losses/ipcw_loss.py`, `metrics/concordance.py`  

**Layman's Explanation:** 
How do we grade the model? In normal ML, you are right or wrong. But what if a patient drops out of the study at year 2? We don't know if they got dementia in year 3. Baseline models struggle with this "censoring". We use special math (IPCW) to give more weight to the patients who *did* stay in the study longer, so the model isn't penalized for patients who drop out early.

**Technical Explanation & Tradeoffs:**
- **Interpolation:** We use Constant Hazard Interpolation (CHI) to draw a continuous curve between our 5 discrete grid points.
- **Loss (IPCW):** We use Inverse Probability of Censoring Weighting. We fit a Kaplan-Meier curve to the *censoring* times. Patients who experience the event at a time when most people have dropped out are highly valuable, so we up-weight their loss `w_i = 1 / G(T_i)`.
- **Metrics:** 
  - *Antolini's $C_{td}$*: Evaluates pairs of patients. If Patient A gets dementia before Patient B, did our model assign Patient A a higher risk?
  - *Integrated Brier Score (IBS)*: Measures the absolute calibration of the survival probabilities.
- **Tradeoffs:** We completely removed `scikit-survival` from our pipeline because it forces an older version of NumPy/PyTorch, which breaks Apple Silicon (MPS) acceleration. Instead, we use `lifelines` and custom NumPy implementations, allowing us to run on native PyTorch 2.11 for massive speedups.

## Phase 9: Pipeline Assembly & LP-FT Training Protocol
**File Locations:** `models/pipeline.py`, `training/trainer.py`  
**Classes:** `ADNISurvivalPipeline`, `LPFTTrainer`

**Layman's Explanation:** 
If you teach an expert new concepts too quickly, they might forget what they already know. Because our ResNet50 (Phase 1) is already pre-trained on medical images, if we just hit "train" on the whole pipeline, the random, uncalibrated errors from the end of the model will propagate backward and destroy the ResNet's knowledge. 
So, we train in two steps:
1. **Stage 1 (Linear Probe):** Freeze the brain extractor. Only train the final survival layers until they know roughly what they are doing.
2. **Stage 2 (Fine-Tune):** Unfreeze everything, turn down the learning rate, and let the whole pipeline fine-tune together.

**Technical Explanation & Tradeoffs:**
- **ADNISurvivalPipeline:** Wires Phase 1 through 6 into one cohesive `nn.Module`.
- **LPFT Protocol:**
  - *Stage 1 (Linear Probe):* Only `PMA` and `SurvivalHead` are trained (LR = 1e-3). Everything else is frozen. Runs for 5 epochs.
  - *Stage 2 (Fine Tuning):* The backbone (ResNet) stays frozen, but the sequence models (Longformer/Mamba), gating, and projection heads are unfrozen with differential learning rates (e.g., Sequence models at 5e-5, Survival head at 1e-3). We use Cosine Decay LR scheduling and Mixed Precision (FP16) via `GradScaler`.
- **Tradeoffs:** LP-FT takes longer to implement and requires careful memory management between stages, but it strictly prevents *catastrophic forgetting* and stabilizes the early training dynamics (preventing NaN losses in the IPCW function).
